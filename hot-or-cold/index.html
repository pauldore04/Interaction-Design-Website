Interaction Design

Sensor Experiment

Using a ESP32 Microcontroller, 1 Sensor of your choice, 1 (RGB)-LED and your individual Code, your group should create an interesting / fun / engaging interactive system.

Group: Frithjof Plath, Tristan Meyne, Paul Doré

Documentation

Our first idea was to build a small basketball hoop and use a light barrier or ultrasonic sensor to detect when a ball passed through the hoop. We ultimately rejected this concept as it had already been implemented in another course.

We then searched through available components for possible inputs, trying to find an unconventional switch or interaction method.

Secondary Concepts

Our second concept was inspired by the "Red Light, Green Light" game:

  • A touch sensor would detect input
  • When the light shows green, players must tap repeatedly to move forward
  • When the light turns red, players must stop immediately
  • If movement is detected during a red light, the player loses

We also considered a reaction game where the lamp would light up on one of four sides of a joystick, and the player would need to move in the opposite direction.

Final Concept: Hot and Cold with Joystick

We ultimately settled on a "Hot and Cold" game using a joystick:

  • The player navigates to find a hidden target position
  • As they get closer to the target, the LED becomes brighter and more red (hot)
  • As they move away, the LED becomes more blue (cold)
  • Pressing the joystick button starts a new game, with the LED briefly flashing green
  • When a player reaches the target (wins), the LED flashes red
  • If a player reaches the edge of the playing field, the LED flashes blue

Technical Implementation

During assembly, we initially thought we had fried the ESP32 microcontroller, but later discovered we had accidentally connected ground to 5V.

While Tristan worked on connecting the ESP32 with PlatformIO, Paul and Frithjof assembled the hardware components.

We set up Teleplot to have a serial plotter in Visual Studio Code. After getting the serial plotter working, we could visualize both X and Y values from the joystick.

We determined the joystick's value ranges:

  • Center position (no movement): 2400
  • Minimum value: 0
  • Maximum value: 3671

We programmed a graphical representation to help us visualize the joystick's current position while developing.

Learning Break

During development, we had a lecture on the history of Interaction Design. The most interesting part was about pigeons being trained to target enemy boats – essentially an early predecessor to training intelligence for specific tasks.

Final Refinements (Friday, April 11)

From 10:00 to 11:30, we had time to make final adjustments to our project. After user testing, we implemented several changes:

  1. We modified the difficulty selection to appear after each game instead of only at the beginning
  2. We changed the winning light indicator from green to flashing orange/red, which made more sense contextually (since players are trying to find the "warmest" spot, or brightest red)
  3. We made aesthetic improvements to our web application

We then presented our project and received positive feedback. Before the critique and reflection session, we observed the other groups' presentations.
